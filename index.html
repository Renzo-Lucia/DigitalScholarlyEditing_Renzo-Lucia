About the project

At the heart of the new project Renzo & Lucia lies a fascinating challenge: to analyse, compare, and digitally map the differences between an original work and its translation into another language. What makes this case even more intriguing and compelling is the fact that this comparison focuses on two highly distinctive 19th-century works.


  • I promessi sposi by Alessandro Manzoni (1827), also called Ventisettana
  • Los novios by Juan Nicasio Gallego (1836-37)

It is worth pointing out that this is not the very first Spanish translation of Manzoni’s 1827 masterpiece. In fact, it is at least the second translation , published in 1836-37, that means around a decade after the original. The earlier 1833 translation remains little known, with scarce and incomplete information available. For this reason, the later version offers a far more solid and fruitful basis for analysis.



The project is designed for a diverse audience that includes students seeking study material, literature enthusiasts, teachers, and anyone interested in discovering new insights and curiosities about I Promessi Sposi.


Both texts are well-known and widely studied, with multiple digital editions already available to the public. Several platforms also exist that allow users to closely examine and work with these texts.

This project aims to go beyond basic comparison by offering a detailed, side-by-side analysis of the two versions. The goal is to observe their similarities but, more importantly, to higlight the differences — structural, linguistic, cultural, and ideological — that emerge between the original and the translation. These differences are made visible through a digital layout that allows the two texts to be viewed in parallel, one next to the other.

What makes this comparison particularly compelling is that the variations are not simply accidental or stylistic. Instead, they reveal how language, culture, and historical context can shape the way a story is told. The translation, therefore, is not just a literal rendering of the original, but rather as heavily influenced by the context and historical period from which the translator comes from anf in which he or she lives.

As a result, this project demonstrates that a translation should be viewed more accurately as a form of cultural adaptation, both linguistic and ideological.
