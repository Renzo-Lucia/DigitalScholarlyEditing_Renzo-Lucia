The writers

Alessandro Manzoni

Who was Alessandro Manzoni?


Alessandro Manzoni was an Italian poet, novelist, and philosopher. He is best known for his novel I promessi sposi (The Betrothed), considered one of the masterpieces of world literature and a symbol of the Italian Risorgimento, both for its patriotic message and for its crucial role in shaping the modern, unified Italian language.

I promessi sposi is an Italian historical novel, first published in 1827 in three volumes, and later extensively revised until the definitive edition, published between 1840 and 1842. It remains one of the most famous and widely read novels in the Italian language.

Actually, Manzoni wrote the first version, Fermo e Lucia, between 1821 and 1823. He then revised it deeply, completing his work in 1825, and after two years of corrections and proofreading, it was published in 1827. In the early 19th century, Manzoni once again revised the novel’s language for the 1842 edition, eliminating many Lombard regionalisms and adopting the Florentine dialect, that he considered the best model for a national literary Italian.

Juan Nicasio Gallego

Who was Juan Nicasio Gallego?


Juan Nicasio Gallego was a prominent Spanish priest, intellectual, poet, and public figure with liberal Catholic ideals. He was well connected to Spain’s literary and religious circles and was widely recognized for his rhetorical style and moral seriousness.
He became a priest in 1804, then a royal chaplain in Madrid in 1805 and served as spiritual director of the Royal Palace pages. During the time he spent in Madrid, he came into contact with pre-Romantic intellectuals and began publishing poetry in the Memorial Literario. During the uprising of May 1808, he wrote Influencia del entusiasmo público en las artes.

He later moved to Seville and then to Cádiz, where he became a constituent deputy for Zamora and worked at the Cortes of Cádiz, contributing to the press freedom law. Persecuted by Ferdinand VII for his liberal ideas, he was imprisoned and later exiled for four years. Once freed in 1820, he held various ecclesiastical and literary positions.

Gallego deeply admired I Promessi Sposi and considered it as both a religious and civic model, aligning with his worldview. He translated Manzoni's The Betrothed into Spanish and was a member of the Royal Spanish Academy.
